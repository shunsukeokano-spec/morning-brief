AI memory crunch deepens as Europe hardens on China tech — and Nvidia's H200 China dream stalls
The global DRAM/HBM shortage is now the worst in 15 years, with AI data centers consuming 70% of memory output and prices up 58–75% this quarter. Meanwhile, the EU is moving to legally ban Huawei and ZTE from all critical infrastructure, triggering Chinese retaliation threats — a flashpoint that directly answers Shun's question about whether Europe is drawing closer to, or further from, China.
Western · IEEE Spectrum / Fortune / WCCFTech World ↓
AI Memory Crunch Hits 15-Year Worst: DRAM Up 63%, Shortage to Outlast 2026 ↗
Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron have systematically reallocated fab capacity toward high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI accelerators, leaving consumer DRAM and NAND in critically short supply. In Q2 2026, DRAM contract prices rose 58–63% and NAND surged 70–75% quarter-over-quarter — the largest increases in a decade. Micron told JPMorgan this week that tightness will persist well beyond 2026, as HBM4 production ramps twice as fast as HBM3 and new fab capacity won't come online until 2027–28.
↳ This is not a cyclical blip — it is a structural reallocation of global silicon capacity toward AI, with cascading price effects on smartphones, PCs, and automotive that will persist through 2027–28, directly impacting Japanese consumer electronics and semiconductor firms.
Western · CNBC / Reuters / Tom's Hardware Your Focus ◉
Nvidia H200 China Sales Stall: Beijing Opts for Domestic Chips Despite US Approval ↗
The US Commerce Department approved roughly 10 Chinese firms — including Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, and Lenovo — to purchase Nvidia's H200 chips. But following the Trump–Xi summit in May, Trump revealed China 'chose not to' sanction the purchases, preferring to develop its own AI chips. Nvidia's $78B revenue guidance assumes zero H200 recovery from China, though analysts estimate a functioning framework would restore $3.5–4B in annual revenue.
↳ Beijing's deliberate rejection of approved US AI chips signals a strategic commitment to domestic semiconductor self-reliance over short-term capability gains — a major indicator that China's tech decoupling is driven by policy choice, not just US export controls.
Western · The Next Web / Light Reading / Reuters Your Focus ◉
EU Moves to Legally Ban Huawei & ZTE — China Threatens 'Decisive' Retaliation ↗
On May 4, 2026, the European Commission formally recommended all 27 EU member states exclude Huawei and ZTE from telecom and critical infrastructure, backed by a new Cybersecurity Act that would mandate a 36-month phase-out with financial penalties for non-compliance. China filed a 30-page rebuttal calling the rules 'discriminatory,' warning it will 'act decisively' if the EU proceeds — with threatened countermeasures targeting German cars, French luxury goods, and Ericsson/Nokia's Chinese market access.
↳ This is the most concrete test yet of whether EU 'de-risking' from China has real teeth — Germany's political will and Italy/Spain's operator dependencies will determine whether the bloc can enforce binding tech sovereignty, or fractures under Chinese economic pressure.
Western · Council on Foreign Relations / MERICS / Euronews Your Focus ◉
Europe–China Sentiment: De-Risking at Brussels Level, Rapprochement at Capital Level — A Deepening Divide ↗
Shun's question — is Europe drawing closer to China? — has a split answer. At the EU level, relations have deteriorated sharply: rare earth export controls, Huawei bans, EV tariffs, and WTO disputes reflect a hardening Brussels posture. But at the national level, UK PM Starmer secured a $4.5B deal reset in January, and German Chancellor Merz visited Beijing in February with 30 top executives, pointedly refusing to call China a 'systemic rival.' MERICS' 2026 forecast found 84% of European experts were pessimistic about the EU's ability to reduce China dependencies, while China's leverage over Europe is assessed as growing.
↳ Europe is not moving uniformly toward or away from China — it is fracturing, with Brussels pushing de-risking while key capitals pursue bilateral engagement. This 'divide-and-rule' dynamic is China's preferred strategic outcome, and has direct implications for how Japan calibrates its own EU–China triangulation.
30–90 Day Signal
Watch three converging pressure points over the next 30–90 days: (1) Whether the EU Cybersecurity Act moves from recommendation to binding vote in the European Parliament — Germany's stance on Huawei's ~60% share of its 5G estate is the swing variable; (2) Whether Nvidia's H200 China impasse triggers a structural pivot by Chinese hyperscalers toward Huawei's Atlas 350 accelerator, which would mark a durable loss of market share for US AI hardware; (3) Whether the global DRAM/HBM shortage cascades into smartphone market contraction — IDC's moderate scenario forecasts a 2.9% decline in 2026 global smartphone shipments, with ASPs rising 3–5%, which will directly pressure Japanese consumer electronics OEMs and Korean memory suppliers with Japan operations (SK Hynix Icheon, Micron Hiroshima).
