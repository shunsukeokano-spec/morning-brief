NVIDIA Launches RTX Spark Superchip at Computex 2026, Declaring War on Intel and AMD in the PC Market ↗
At Computex 2026 in Taipei, Jensen Huang unveiled the RTX Spark — an ARM-based superchip pairing a 20-core Grace CPU with a 6,144-core Blackwell GPU and 128GB unified memory, designed to turn Windows PCs into agentic AI platforms. The chip, co-developed with MediaTek and Microsoft, will debut in fall 2026 across 30+ laptops from Dell, HP, ASUS, Lenovo, and MSI, priced from $1,799. NVIDIA simultaneously confirmed its Vera data center CPU is in full production, with early customers including OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX's xAI — signalling a full-stack play from PC to hyperscale.
↳ NVIDIA's vertical expansion from data centre dominance into PC silicon marks the most significant reshaping of the consumer computing landscape since Apple's M1 transition — and directly threatens Intel's last major revenue fortress.

US Closes AI Chip Export Loophole After Chinese Firms Quietly Acquired Blackwell Chips via Overseas Subsidiaries for a Year ↗
The US Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security issued emergency guidance clarifying that export licence requirements apply to advanced chips — including NVIDIA Blackwell and AMD MI350X — sold to any entity headquartered in China, regardless of where that entity operates. The loophole, open for roughly a year after the Trump administration declined to enforce Biden-era AI diffusion rules, may have allowed hundreds of thousands of chips to reach Chinese-linked subsidiaries in Malaysia and elsewhere. China's Commerce Ministry responded by publicly accusing the US of 'abusing export controls' and harming global supply chain stability, escalating the rhetoric of the chip war.
↳ The loophole closure tightens the AI hardware blockade on China just as NVIDIA's most powerful generation of chips is shipping at scale — accelerating Beijing's urgency to domesticate its chip stack while creating compliance complexity for Southeast Asian data centre hubs like Malaysia that sit in the middle.

WSTS Revises 2026 Semiconductor Market Forecast to $1.51 Trillion — a 90% Annual Surge, the Largest in Industry History ↗
The World Semiconductor Trade Statistics organisation released its Spring 2026 forecast on June 2nd, projecting the global chip market will reach $1.51 trillion in 2026 — an 89.9% year-on-year increase and the first time the market has surpassed $1 trillion, smashing the prior record growth rate of 42% set in 1995. The revision was driven almost entirely by memory semiconductors, forecast to surge 250% to $803.9 billion as HBM demand from AI data centres explodes. Japan's semiconductor revenue is projected to grow 27.6% in 2026 to $57.1 billion, with Kioxia Holdings flagged as a key beneficiary. WSTS further projects the market will reach $1.9 trillion in 2027.
↳ A 90% single-year market expansion — driven entirely by AI infrastructure — confirms that the semiconductor super-cycle is not a projection but a present reality, with direct tailwinds for Japanese memory players and TSMC, and growing supply-side risks as HBM and advanced packaging remain structural bottlenecks.

China's Domestic Chip Firms Hit Record Revenue as US Restrictions Paradoxically Accelerate Self-Sufficiency Drive ↗
SMIC, China's largest chipmaker, recorded $9.3 billion in revenue for 2025 — a 16% increase — with analysts projecting it could top $11 billion in 2026. Memory player ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT) saw a 130% revenue surge to over $8 billion. While China remains roughly two generations behind in HBM technology, Huawei's Ascend 910C accelerator is gaining traction among Chinese enterprises, and Beijing's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) targets breakthroughs in advanced logic nodes, HBM development, and domestic EDA tools — aiming for sufficient AI self-sufficiency for its domestic market without needing to match TSMC.
↳ The US chip war is producing the opposite of its intended effect at the domestic level: Chinese firms are posting record revenues, accelerating indigenisation, and reducing NVIDIA dependency by design — a bifurcation that will define the global AI infrastructure landscape over the next 5–10 years.
